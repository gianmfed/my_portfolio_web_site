Road Signs Recognizer

With my magic-eye CNN, you'll never miss a stop sign or speed limit again!.

Introducing the cutting-edge technology that will revolutionize the way we navigate our roads: The "Road Wizard" CNN. This bad boy is trained to recognize road signs with near-magical precision, leaving human error in the rear-view mirror.

The goal of this project is to develop a CNN-based model for accurately recognizing road signs in real-world environments. The model is trained using the German Traffic Sign Recognition Benchmark (GTSRB) dataset of images of road signs, including different types of signs such as stop signs, speed limit signs, and yield signs. The CNN will learn to identify the key features of each type of sign and use this knowledge to make predictions on new images.

Q-Player

The AI is so good at playing games, it could even make 'rock-paper-scissors' interesting again!

The AI program developed is capable of playing games using Q-learning, a type of reinforcement learning algorithm. The algorithm allows the AI to learn to make decisions by maximizing the expected future reward. This means that the AI can learn to play a game by simply playing it repeatedly, without any prior knowledge of the game's rules or strategies.

One example of a game that the AI has been able to play is Nim, a two-player mathematical game of strategy. The AI has demonstrated proficiency in playing Nim at a high level by using Q-learning to learn the optimal strategies for the game.

Checkout Classifier

This AI program is like a fortune teller for online shopping, using a fancy math trick called the nearest-neighbor classifier to predict whether customers will complete a purchase or not, by comparing their browsing behavior to a dataset of 12,000 previous shoppers like a crystal ball.

The program utilizes a nearest-neighbor classifier, a simple, non-parametric method for classification, to predict whether online shoppers will complete a purchase. The classifier uses information about the user such as the number of pages visited, whether they're shopping on a weekend, and their web browser, to find the most similar observation in the training data and make a prediction about whether the user will complete a purchase or not. The classifier is trained on a dataset of approximately 12,000 user sessions from a shopping website.

This program can be useful for the e-commerce website to identify the factors that affect the customer's buying decision and take necessary actions.
